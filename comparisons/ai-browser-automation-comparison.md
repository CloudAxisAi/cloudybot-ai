## Detailed Breakdown

### CloudyBot
**Best for:** Non-technical founders, sales teams, ops managers.

**Strengths:** Zero setup, handles unstructured tasks, WhatsApp interface, hard-capped pricing, built-in knowledge base.

**Limitations:** Not ideal for high-volume programmatic workflows. API on higher plans only.

**Website:** [cloudybot.ai](https://cloudybot.ai)

---

### Browserbase
**Best for:** Developers building products that need cloud browsers at scale.

**Strengths:** Extremely scalable, full programmatic control, Playwright/Puppeteer compatible, anti-detection features.

**Limitations:** Requires coding. No AI layer. Usage-based pricing (no hard cap).

---

### Make (formerly Integromat)
**Best for:** Structured, repeatable workflows between known apps.

**Strengths:** Huge app library, visual builder, good for data transformation.

**Limitations:** Not an AI agent — can't handle open-ended tasks. No cloud browser. Brittle when website structures change.

---

### Zapier
**Best for:** Simple two-step integrations between popular apps.

**Strengths:** Largest app library, very easy to set up, reliable for simple automations.

**Limitations:** Limited logic. No browser automation. No AI context understanding. Gets expensive at scale.

---

### Playwright / Puppeteer
**Best for:** Developers who need full custom control.

**Strengths:** Free, open source, maximum flexibility, large community.

**Limitations:** Requires coding and infrastructure management. Sites actively detect and block these libraries.

## The Real Difference: Rules vs Goals

Most tools here are rule-based — they do exactly what you configure.

CloudyBot is an AI agent — you give it a goal in plain English, it figures out the steps. That's the distinction between automation (rules) and an agent (goals).
